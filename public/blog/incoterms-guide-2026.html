What Are Incoterms and Why Do They Matter?

Incoterms (International Commercial Terms) are a set of 11 standardized trade terms published by the International Chamber of Commerce (ICC). They define exactly when the risk of loss transfers from seller to buyer, who pays for each leg of the journey, and who handles customs clearance at each end.

For Indian exporters, getting Incoterms wrong is one of the most expensive mistakes you can make. Consider this: an exporter in Ludhiana quotes FOB JNPT prices to a buyer in Rotterdam. The buyer assumes ocean freight is included (which it is not under FOB). By the time the misunderstanding is discovered, the shipment is already at port and the buyer demands a price reduction. The exporter absorbs the freight cost, wiping out their entire margin.

The current version is Incoterms 2020, published by the ICC. There is no "Incoterms 2026" revision, but these terms remain in force and are used universally in 2026 trade. Always reference Incoterms® 2020 in your contracts.

Key Principle

Incoterms determine three things: (1) who arranges and pays for transport, (2) where risk transfers from seller to buyer, and (3) who handles customs clearance. Always specify the Incoterm and the named place in your contract, e.g., "FOB JNPT" not just "FOB."

Incoterms for Any Mode of Transport (7 Terms)

These 7 terms can be used regardless of whether you are shipping by sea, air, rail, road, or multimodal. They are grouped together because risk transfers at a defined point (not at a ship's rail).

EXW
Ex Works

Seller's obligation: Makes goods available at their premises (factory, warehouse, or dock). That is all.

Buyer's obligation: Everything else — loading, export clearance, transport, insurance, import clearance.

Risk transfers: When goods are placed at the buyer's disposal at the named place.

Customs: Buyer handles both export and import clearance.

Example: A textile manufacturer in Tiruppur tells a German buyer, "EXW Tiruppur." The German buyer must arrange a truck from Tiruppur to Chennai port, handle Indian export customs, book the ocean freight, and clear goods at Hamburg. Maximum risk and cost for the buyer — minimum for the seller.
FCA
Free Carrier

Seller's obligation: Delivers goods cleared for export to the carrier nominated by the buyer at a named place.

Buyer's obligation: Main carriage (ocean/air freight), insurance, import clearance.

Risk transfers: When goods are handed to the first carrier.

Customs: Seller handles export clearance; buyer handles import clearance.

Example: A spices exporter in Kochi uses "FCA Kochi Airport." They deliver goods to the air cargo terminal, handle export customs, and hand over the air waybill. The buyer's freight forwarder takes it from there to Dubai.
CPT
Carriage Paid To

Seller's obligation: Pays freight to the named destination but does not insure the goods.

Buyer's obligation: Insurance, import clearance at destination.

Risk transfers: When goods are handed to the first carrier (not at destination).

Customs: Seller handles export clearance; buyer handles import clearance.

Example: "CPT London" — the Indian exporter pays freight from JNPT to Felixstowe port. But risk transferred at JNPT when goods were loaded onto the vessel. If goods are damaged mid-ocean, the buyer bears the loss unless they have insurance.
CIP
Carriage and Insurance Paid To

Seller's obligation: Same as CPT, plus the seller must procure cargo insurance.

Buyer's obligation: Import clearance at destination.

Key difference from CPT: Under Incoterms 2020, CIP requires the seller to purchase All Risks insurance (ICC A), which is more comprehensive than the minimum required under CIF.

Example: "CIP Frankfurt" — an engineering goods exporter in Pune pays freight and insurance all the way to Frankfurt. Risk still transfers at the origin carrier, but the buyer is protected by the seller's insurance policy.
DAP
Delivered at Place

Seller's obligation: Delivers goods to the named destination, ready for unloading. Seller bears all transport costs and risks up to that point.

Buyer's obligation: Unloading at destination, import clearance, import duties and taxes.

Risk transfers: When goods arrive at the named destination, ready for unloading.

Example: "DAP Milan" — an auto parts manufacturer in Gurugram arranges and pays for the entire journey from their factory to the buyer's warehouse in Milan. The Italian buyer only handles import customs and unloading.
DPU
Delivered at Place Unloaded

Seller's obligation: Same as DAP, but the seller also unloads the goods at the destination.

Buyer's obligation: Import clearance, import duties and taxes.

Key difference from DAP: Seller is responsible for unloading. Only Incoterm where seller must unload.

Example: "DPU Jeddah Port" — the Indian exporter ships chemicals from Mumbai, clears export customs, and arranges for the goods to be unloaded at Jeddah port. The Saudi buyer handles import clearance and onward transport.
DDP
Delivered Duty Paid

Seller's obligation: Delivers goods to the buyer's premises, fully cleared for import with all duties and taxes paid. Maximum seller obligation.

Buyer's obligation: Unloading at their premises (unless agreed otherwise).

Risk transfers: When goods are made available at the buyer's premises, cleared for import.

Customs: Seller handles BOTH export and import clearance.

Example: "DDP Berlin" — a leather goods exporter in Kanpur handles everything: trucking to JNPT, ocean freight to Hamburg, truck to Berlin, German import customs, duty payment, and delivery to the buyer's door. The buyer simply receives the goods.
DDP Warning for Indian Exporters

DDP is the riskiest Incoterm for sellers. You are responsible for import clearance in a foreign country. If you are not familiar with the importing country's customs regulations, duties, or documentation requirements, you risk delays, penalties, and unexpected costs. Use DDP only when you have a trusted logistics partner in the destination country.

Incoterms for Sea & Inland Waterway Only (4 Terms)

These 4 terms can ONLY be used when goods are transported by sea or inland waterway. They all involve a ship, and risk transfers at the ship's rail (or when goods are placed on board).

FAS
Free Alongside Ship

Seller's obligation: Delivers goods alongside the vessel at the named port of shipment. Export clearance is the seller's responsibility.

Buyer's obligation: Loading onto the vessel, ocean freight, insurance, import clearance.

Risk transfers: When goods are placed alongside the ship at the port of loading.

Example: "FAS Kandla" — a salt exporter delivers bulk bags to the quay at Kandla port. From there, the buyer arranges loading onto the vessel and all onward transport.
FOB
Free on Board

Seller's obligation: Delivers goods on board the vessel at the named port of shipment. Export clearance is the seller's responsibility.

Buyer's obligation: Ocean freight, insurance, import clearance.

Risk transfers: When goods are on board the vessel.

Key note: The most commonly used Incoterm by Indian exporters. Under Incoterms 2020, risk transfers when goods are on board, not at the ship's rail as in earlier versions.

Example: "FOB JNPT" — a pharmaceutical company in Mumbai loads generics onto a vessel at JNPT. Once on board, the risk passes to the European buyer who arranged the ocean freight to Antwerp. The Indian seller has completed their obligation.
CFR
Cost and Freight

Seller's obligation: Pays ocean freight to the named destination port. But does NOT insure the goods.

Buyer's obligation: Insurance, import clearance at destination.

Risk transfers: When goods are on board at the port of shipment (same as FOB).

Example: "CFR Hamburg" — the Indian exporter pays freight from Nhava Sheva to Hamburg. If a storm damages the cargo mid-ocean, the buyer must claim under their own insurance. Risk transferred at loading in India.
CIF
Cost, Insurance and Freight

Seller's obligation: Pays ocean freight AND procures minimum insurance (ICC C or Institute Cargo Clauses C) to the named destination port.

Buyer's obligation: Import clearance at destination.

Risk transfers: When goods are on board at the port of shipment (same as FOB/CFR).

Key note: Despite paying insurance, risk transfers at origin. The insurance protects the buyer, but the seller only needs minimum coverage. If the buyer wants All Risks coverage, they should negotiate CIP or arrange their own top-up policy.

Example: "CIF Rotterdam" — a rice exporter in Kolkata pays freight and buys ICC C insurance from Kolkata to Rotterdam. If goods are damaged, the buyer claims under the seller's insurance. But note: only minimum coverage is required under CIF.
CIF vs CIP — The Insurance Gap

Under Incoterms 2020, CIF requires only minimum insurance (Institute Cargo Clauses C), while CIP requires All Risks coverage (Institute Cargo Clauses A). For high-value shipments, the difference is significant. If you are buying CIF, consider purchasing additional insurance to cover the gap.

Most Common Incoterms Used by Indian MSMEs

Based on trade data and our experience with Indian exporters, these are the four Incoterms you will encounter most frequently:

1. FOB (Free on Board) — The Workhorse

Used by ~60% of Indian MSME exporters. FOB is the default for sea freight shipments because it gives both parties clear boundaries. The seller loads the goods on the vessel; the buyer arranges ocean freight and insurance.

  • Why Indian MSMEs prefer it: You control the local logistics (trucking to port, loading) and don't have to deal with international freight negotiations
  • Best for: Established buyer relationships where the buyer has their own freight contracts
  • Risk: If the buyer's freight forwarder delays at port, you may face demurrage if goods are not loaded promptly

2. CIF (Cost, Insurance & Freight) — The Convenience Play

Used by ~25% of Indian MSME exporters. Under CIF, you arrange and pay for ocean freight and insurance to the destination port. Risk still transfers at the origin port.

  • Why Indian MSMEs use it: Buyers (especially in Africa, Middle East, and South Asia) prefer a single landed cost. It makes your price more competitive in these markets
  • Best for: New buyer relationships, markets where buyers lack freight expertise
  • Risk: You need to manage freight rates and insurance costs. A sudden freight rate increase can erase your margin

3. EXW (Ex Works) — The Simplest (and Riskiest for Buyers)

Used by ~10% of Indian MSME exporters. You make the goods available at your factory and everything else is the buyer's problem.

  • Why Indian MSMEs use it: When the buyer has a freight forwarder in India, or when the seller doesn't want to handle any logistics
  • Best for: Small-value orders, repeat buyers with established India logistics
  • Risk: Export clearance is technically the buyer's responsibility, but Indian regulations require the IEC holder (seller) to file the Shipping Bill. This creates a practical problem if the buyer doesn't have an Indian entity
EXW Export Clearance Issue

Under EXW, the buyer is supposed to handle export clearance. However, Indian customs requires the Shipping Bill to be filed under the seller's IEC. In practice, most Indian sellers end up assisting with export clearance even under EXW. Clarify this in your contract to avoid disputes.

4. DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) — Premium Service

Used by ~5% of Indian MSME exporters. You handle everything from your factory to the buyer's door, including import duties and clearance in the destination country.

  • Why Indian MSMEs use it: Commands a premium price. Some sellers use it as a competitive advantage when buyers want a hassle-free experience
  • Best for: High-value goods, established logistics in the destination market, B2C exports
  • Risk: You are responsible for import clearance in a foreign country. Any miscalculation on duties or customs delays become your problem

How to Choose the Right Incoterm

There is no single "best" Incoterm. The right choice depends on your relationship with the buyer, your logistics capabilities, and your risk appetite. Here is a decision framework:

Choose EXW if:

  • You want the simplest possible sale — just hand over the goods
  • The buyer has an established logistics setup in India
  • You are uncomfortable with freight negotiations or customs clearance

Choose FOB if:

  • You want control over local logistics (trucking, port handling)
  • The buyer has their own freight contracts and prefers to manage shipping
  • You are exporting sea freight in standard container shipments

Choose CIF if:

  • You want to offer a competitive landed cost to the buyer
  • You have good relationships with freight forwarders and can negotiate competitive rates
  • The buyer is in a developing market where freight expertise is limited

Choose DAP or DDP if:

  • You want to offer a premium, door-to-door service
  • You have reliable logistics partners in the destination country
  • You are confident in handling import regulations in the buyer's country
Negotiation Tip

If a buyer insists on EXW but you prefer FOB, offer a small discount on FOB pricing to make it attractive. The buyer gets door-to-port service and you get to control the local logistics, which is where most delays and problems occur for Indian exporters.

Common Incoterms Mistakes Indian Exporters Make

1. Not Specifying the Named Place

"FOB Mumbai" is incomplete. It should be "FOB JNPT" or "FOB Gateway Port, Nhava Sheva." The named place determines where risk transfers and costs end. Without it, disputes are inevitable.

2. Using Sea-Only Terms for Air Shipments

FOB, CIF, CFR, and FAS can ONLY be used for sea or inland waterway transport. If you are shipping by air, use FCA, CPT, or CIP instead. Using FOB for an air shipment creates a legally ambiguous contract.

3. Confusing CIF Insurance Coverage

Under CIF, you are only required to buy minimum insurance (Institute Cargo Clauses C). Many Indian exporters either buy too much insurance (increasing costs) or too little (leaving buyers exposed). Understand exactly what the Incoterm requires.

4. Assuming Incoterms Handle Payment Terms

Incoterms define risk, cost, and logistics responsibility. They do NOT define when or how payment happens. A CIF contract can be LC, CAD, or open account. Payment terms are a separate negotiation entirely.

5. Not Updating Contracts After Incoterms 2020

If your standard contracts still reference Incoterms 2010, update them. While most terms are the same, there are differences (e.g., FCA can now be accompanied by an on-board notation on the B/L, which was not available in 2010).

6. Mixing Incoterms with Local Currency Pricing

When quoting in INR with Incoterms, clarify who bears the forex risk. Under FOB JNPT priced in USD, the seller bears INR/USD fluctuation risk until shipment. Make this explicit in your contract.

Contract Language

Always use the exact Incoterm abbreviation + named place + Incoterms® version. Example: "FOB JNPT, Nhava Sheva, India, Incoterms® 2020." This eliminates ambiguity and is legally enforceable.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use Incoterms for domestic trade within India?
Incoterms are designed for international trade. For domestic transactions within India, they are not legally applicable. However, some Indian MSMEs informally use Incoterms to clarify logistics responsibility between parties in different states. For domestic trade, use GST-compliant invoices and clearly define delivery terms in your sales agreement.
What happens if the buyer and seller disagree on which Incoterm to use?
The Incoterm is always negotiated and must be agreed upon by both parties before the contract is signed. It cannot be unilaterally changed. If you are quoting to a buyer who insists on EXW but you prefer FOB, negotiate a price adjustment that accounts for the logistics difference. Document the agreed Incoterm in your Purchase Order, Proforma Invoice, and Sales Contract.
Does the Incoterm affect my RoDTEP eligibility?
Your RoDTEP eligibility is based on the HS code and FOB value of your goods, not on the Incoterm used. However, the Incoterm affects how the FOB value is calculated on your Shipping Bill. Under CIF or CFR terms, the freight and insurance components are deducted to arrive at the FOB value for RoDTEP purposes. Ensure your Customs House Agent correctly calculates this to avoid under-claiming your RoDTEP refund.
