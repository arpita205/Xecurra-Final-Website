Why Documentation Matters for MSME Exporters

India's export target of $2 Trillion by 2030 is being driven largely by MSMEs. But for every shipment that clears customs smoothly, there are hundreds delayed or rejected because of incorrect paperwork.

Export documentation isn't bureaucracy for its own sake — it's the mechanism that ensures your goods get preferential duty rates, comply with importing country regulations, clear Indian customs, and get you paid by your buyer. A single error in your HS code or a missing stamp on your shipping bill can cost you days of demurrage, thousands in penalties, or worse — a lost buyer.

This checklist covers every document category you need to master, with specific portal references and practical tips you can use immediately.

Quick Tip

Bookmark this page. Export documentation requirements change frequently, and we keep this updated as government policies evolve through 2026.

Pre-Shipment Documents (Set Up Before You Export)

These are the foundational registrations and licences you need before you can legally export from India. Without them, you cannot even open a shipping bill on ICEGATE.

1. Import Export Code (IEC)

The IEC is a 10-digit alphanumeric code issued by the Directorate General of Foreign Trade (DGFT). It is mandatory for every exporter — no IEC, no export. Period.

  • Apply online at dgft.gov.in
  • Fee: ₹500 (online payment)
  • Processing time: 1–2 working days (auto-generated in most cases)
  • Validity: Lifetime — but you must update it if your business details change
  • Required: PAN card, cancelled cheque, digital photograph, and address proof
Important

Since 2024, IEC is linked to your PAN. Ensure your PAN card has your current business name and address. Any mismatch will cause IEC issuance to fail on DGFT.

2. GST Registration

Even if your turnover is below the GST threshold, exporters must have a GST registration to claim duty drawback and Input Tax Credit (ITC) refunds.

  • Register at gst.gov.in
  • Select Export of Goods as your business activity during registration
  • You will need your IEC number during GST registration
  • Exports are zero-rated under GST — you do not pay GST on exports, but you can claim refund of input taxes

3. AD Code Registration (Authorized Dealer Code)

The AD Code is the IFSC code of the bank branch where you want to receive your export proceeds. It must be registered with Indian Customs (via ICEGATE) before you can file a Shipping Bill.

  • Obtain AD Code letter from your bank
  • Register it on ICEGATE under your IEC
  • Processing time: 2–7 working days
  • Only one AD Code can be active per IEC at a time

4. RCMC (Registration Cum Membership Certificate)

Issued by your relevant Export Promotion Council (EPC), the RCMC proves your membership and is needed to claim benefits under FTP schemes like RoDTEP.

  • Apply to the EPC relevant to your product (e.g., EEPC for engineering, FIEO for general, APEDA for agri products)
  • Fee varies by EPC (typically ₹2,000–₹5,000/year)
  • You will need: IEC copy, GST certificate, company PAN, product details
Pre-Shipment Checklist
  • IEC registered and active on DGFT
  • GST registration with export activity enabled
  • AD Code registered on ICEGATE
  • RCMC obtained from relevant Export Promotion Council
  • PAN card updated with current business name
  • Digital Signature Certificate (DSC) for ICEGATE filings

Shipping Documents (Per Shipment)

Every individual export shipment requires a specific set of documents. These travel with the cargo and are needed for customs clearance at both ends.

1. Commercial Invoice

The primary document that establishes the value of goods being exported. Customs uses this to assess duties (if applicable) and it forms the basis for your foreign exchange receipt.

  • Must include: Seller and buyer details, IEC number, product description, quantity, unit price, total value, currency, Incoterms, country of origin
  • Must match your Shipping Bill exactly — name mismatches are the number one cause of clearance delays
  • Keep at least 3 originals for documentary collection transactions

2. Packing List

Details the contents of each package in the shipment. Customs officers and your buyer both rely on this to verify what is inside.

  • Must include: Number of packages, gross and net weight (in Kgs), dimensions, contents per package, marks and numbers on packages
  • Weight must match what is declared on your Shipping Bill and Bill of Lading

3. Shipping Bill

This is the king of export documents — it is the declaration you file on ICEGATE that allows your cargo to leave India.

  • Filed electronically via ICEGATE using your DSC and IEC
  • Contains: IEC, AD Code, HS codes, product details, destination country, CIF value, duty drawback details
  • Types: Standard Shipping Bill, Green Channel Shipping Bill, Bill of Export
  • Processing: Customs typically processes within 24 hours if documentation is correct
Pro Tip

Use ICEGATE's Facility for Filing Shipping Bill module. It auto-populates several fields from your IEC profile, reducing manual entry errors. Ensure your IEC profile on DGFT has updated exporter category and address.

4. Bill of Lading (Sea) / Airway Bill (Air)

The transport document issued by the carrier (shipping line or airline). It serves as:

  • Receipt of goods by the carrier
  • Evidence of the contract of carriage
  • Document of title to the goods (in case of Bill of Lading)
  • For sea freight: Bill of Lading (B/L) — you will need the original for LC transactions
  • For air freight: Air Waybill (AWB) — non-negotiable

5. Insurance Certificate / Policy

Required for most CIF (Cost, Insurance, Freight) shipments and all Letter of Credit transactions.

  • Must cover at least 110% of the CIF value
  • Should be issued by a reputable insurer recognized in India
  • Coverage type: Institute Cargo Clauses (A) or (B) depending on buyer requirements

6. Letter of Credit Documents

If you are exporting under a Letter of Credit (LC), your bank will require:

  • Commercial Invoice (as per LC terms — verify every clause)
  • Bill of Lading / Air Waybill
  • Insurance Certificate / Policy
  • Packing List
  • Certificate of Origin (if required by LC)
  • Any other documents specified in the LC (inspection certificates, fumigation certificates, etc.)
LC Document Discrepancies

According to ICC data, roughly 70% of first-presented LCs have discrepancies. The most common: name mismatches between invoice and B/L, missing insurance cover, and late presentation. Triple-check every document against your LC terms before submission.

Shipping Documents Checklist
  • Commercial Invoice (matching Shipping Bill exactly)
  • Packing List (weights verified)
  • Shipping Bill (filed on ICEGATE)
  • Bill of Lading / Air Waybill
  • Insurance Certificate (if CIF terms)
  • LC documents (if applicable)

Compliance & Regulatory Documents

Depending on what you are exporting and where, you may need additional compliance documents. These are product-specific and destination-specific.

Certificate of Origin (CoO)

Proves your goods were manufactured or processed in India. Required by the importing country for tariff benefits.

  • Non-Preferential CoO: Issued by Chambers of Commerce (FICCI, CII, or your local Chamber). Proves goods originate in India.
  • Preferential CoO: Required under Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) like India-UAE CEPA, India-Australia ECTA, India-Singapore CECA. Gives your goods reduced or zero duty in the importing country.
  • Apply via the electronic Certificate of Origin (eCoO) portal at ecooicegate.gov.in
  • Processing: 1–3 working days

Phytosanitary Certificate

Mandatory for agricultural products, food items, wood, and plant-based materials. Issued by the Plant Quarantine Authority of India under the Plant Quarantine Order, 2003.

  • Apply through the Plant Quarantine portal or the nearest PQ station
  • Inspection of goods is mandatory before issuance
  • Required by most importing countries for agri products (EU, US, Japan, Australia)

FSSAI License / Certificate

Required if you are exporting food products from India. The Food Safety and Standards Authority of India (FSSAI) ensures your food meets domestic safety standards.

  • Apply at fssai.gov.in
  • Types: State FSSAI License (turnover up to ₹12 crore) or Central FSSAI License (above ₹12 crore)
  • Many importing countries (EU, Japan) require FSSAI compliance as a prerequisite

Fumigation Certificate

Required for wood packaging materials, timber, and certain agricultural products. Proves the goods have been treated to prevent pest infestation.

  • Must comply with ISPM 15 (International Standards for Phytosanitary Measures)
  • Issued by approved fumigation agencies
  • Required by most countries for wooden pallets and crates

Lab Test / Inspection Certificates

Many product categories require third-party or government lab testing before export:

  • BIS Certification: For products covered under compulsory BIS standards (electricals, cement, steel)
  • Textile testing: Bureau of Indian Standards testing for garments and textiles
  • Chemical testing: For pharmaceuticals and chemicals — CDSCO approval may be required
  • Pre-shipment inspection: Required by some importing countries (e.g., Iran's PSI mandate)
Compliance Documents Checklist
  • Certificate of Origin (Non-Preferential or Preferential as needed)
  • Phytosanitary Certificate (for agri/plant products)
  • FSSAI License (for food exports)
  • Fumigation Certificate (for wood packaging)
  • Lab test / inspection certificates (product-specific)
  • Drug manufacturing license (for pharma exports)

Financial Documents (Getting Paid)

Getting your money out of the deal requires careful documentation. These documents ensure you receive foreign exchange and can claim all applicable government benefits.

Letter of Credit (LC)

The most secure payment method for Indian MSMEs. Your buyer's bank guarantees payment upon presentation of conforming documents.

  • Apply for LC confirmation through your AD bank
  • Understand the difference: Confirmed LC (bank adds its guarantee) vs. Unconfirmed LC (only buyer's bank guarantees)
  • Key terms to verify: Expiry date, presentation period, partial shipments, transshipment allowed
  • Typical processing: 3–5 working days for LC advising

Bank Realisation Certificate (BRC)

A critical document issued by your AD bank confirming that foreign exchange has been received against your export. Without BRC, you cannot:

  • Claim RoDTEP (Remission of Duties and Taxes on Exported Products) refund
  • Claim Duty Drawback
  • File for GST refund on exported goods
  • Prove export realization to DGFT for FTP benefits

BRC is generated automatically when your bank receives and processes the foreign exchange remittance. Monitor it on ICEGATE.

Foreign Exchange Realization

Key points MSMEs need to understand about forex receipts:

  • Timeline: Export proceeds must be realized within 9 months from the date of export (as per RBI's Liberalised Remittance Scheme)
  • Currency: You can receive in any freely convertible currency — USD, EUR, GBP, AED, etc.
  • Forward contracts: Use forex forward contracts to hedge against currency fluctuation risk
  • Reporting: Your bank will report the realization to RBI and generate BRC on ICEGATE

RoDTEP Claim Documents

The RoDTEP scheme refunds embedded central, state, and local taxes/duties on exported goods. To claim it, you need:

  • Shipping Bill with RoDTEP endorsement (auto-calculated by Customs)
  • BRC from your AD bank
  • RCMC from your Export Promotion Council
  • Claim is filed through ICEGATE — typically credited within 30–45 days
Pro Tip

RoDTEP rates vary by HS code and are updated annually. Check the latest schedule on the CBIC website before quoting prices to your buyer. Some HS codes get 0% RoDTEP, which directly impacts your export margin.

Financial Documents Checklist
  • Letter of Credit (if LC terms)
  • Bank Realisation Certificate (auto-generated on forex receipt)
  • Foreign exchange realization within 9-month RBI deadline
  • RoDTEP claim filed via ICEGATE
  • Duty Drawback application (if applicable)
  • GST refund claim for zero-rated exports

Common Mistakes to Avoid

These are the errors that MSMEs make most frequently. Each one can delay your shipment, cost you money, or damage your relationship with buyers.

1. HS Code Errors

The Harmonized System (HS) code classifies your product for tariff purposes. Wrong HS code means:

  • Wrong duty rates applied (you overpay or underpay)
  • RoDTEP refund miscalculated
  • Potential rejection of your Shipping Bill by Customs
  • Buyer's country may apply higher import duty on misclassified goods

Always verify your HS code on the CBIC website and cross-check with your buyer's import requirements. The first 6 digits are international; digits 7–10 are India-specific.

2. Mismatched Names Across Documents

This is the number one cause of document discrepancies. Your company name, buyer name, product description, and address must be identical across all documents.

  • Commercial Invoice must match Shipping Bill
  • Shipping Bill must match Bill of Lading
  • Buyer name on invoice must match LC beneficiary name
  • Address details must be consistent (even abbreviations matter)

3. Missing Stamps and Signatures

Physical documents still need:

  • Authorized signatory stamp and signature on Commercial Invoice
  • Company stamp on Packing List
  • Customs endorsement stamp on Shipping Bill (auto-applied for electronic filing)
  • Chamber of Commerce stamp on Certificate of Origin

4. Weight and Quantity Discrepancies

If your Packing List says 1,000 Kgs but your Shipping Bill says 980 Kgs, Customs will flag it. Weights must be consistent across:

  • Packing List
  • Shipping Bill
  • Bill of Lading / Air Waybill
  • Commercial Invoice

5. Late Filing

Some documents have strict deadlines:

  • Shipping Bill must be filed before cargo is loaded
  • BRC must be obtained within 9 months of export date
  • RoDTEP claims must be filed within the stipulated time frame
  • GST refund claims have time limits under GST law
Penalty Alert

Under the Foreign Trade Policy 2023, penalties for incorrect declarations on Shipping Bills can range from ₹10,000 to ₹5,00,000 depending on the severity. Repeated violations can lead to suspension of your IEC.
