Why Customs Clearance Matters for Every Shipment

Whether you are exporting leather goods from Kanpur or importing electronics from Shenzhen, customs clearance is the gatekeeper. It is the process by which goods are legally allowed to cross India's borders, and it involves documentation verification, duty assessment, and regulatory compliance.

India cleared over $1.2 trillion worth of trade through customs in 2025, and that number continues to grow. Yet customs-related delays remain one of the top three pain points for Indian MSMEs. A Shipping Bill with an incorrect HS code, a missing AD Code registration, or a mismatched invoice can hold up your cargo for days, costing you demurrage, detention, and potentially your buyer's trust.

This guide walks you through the entire customs clearance process — from the registrations you need before you can even file a Shipping Bill, to the digital systems that now handle most interactions, to the duty structure you need to understand for pricing. Whether you are a first-time exporter or an experienced trader, this is your reference for 2026.

2026 Update

Indian Customs has expanded its Faceless Assessment program to more ports in 2026. This means your Shipping Bill may be assessed by an officer in a different city than your port of export. Ensure your documentation is impeccable — there is no "friendly" local officer to clarify things with anymore.

Pre-Shipment Process: Registrations You Need

Before you can file a single Shipping Bill on ICEGATE, you need three critical registrations. These are non-negotiable, and getting them wrong will delay every shipment you make.

1. Import Export Code (IEC)

The IEC is your 10-digit identity in India's foreign trade system. Without it, you cannot export or import — period.

  • Where to apply: DGFT Online Portal
  • Fee: ₹500 (online payment via net banking/UPI)
  • Processing time: 1–2 working days (auto-generated in most cases)
  • Validity: Lifetime — but must be updated if PAN, address, or business name changes
  • Documents needed: PAN card, cancelled cheque, digital photograph, address proof
PAN-IEC Link

Your IEC is linked to your PAN. If your PAN card shows an old business name or address, your IEC application will be rejected. Update your PAN first on the NSDL/UTIITSL portal before applying for IEC.

2. AD Code (Authorized Dealer Code)

The AD Code is the IFSC code of the bank branch where you want to receive your export proceeds. Indian Customs requires this to be registered before you can file a Shipping Bill.

  • Step 1: Obtain an AD Code letter from your bank (the branch where your export account is held)
  • Step 2: Register the AD Code on ICEGATE under your IEC
  • Processing time: 2–7 working days
  • Restriction: Only one AD Code can be active per IEC at any time. Changing it requires de-registration of the old code first
Pro Tip

If you bank with multiple branches, register the AD Code of the branch where you want to receive foreign exchange. Changing it later requires re-registration, which can delay your shipments by 3–5 days.

3. RCMC (Registration Cum Membership Certificate)

The RCMC is issued by your relevant Export Promotion Council (EPC) and is required to claim benefits under the Foreign Trade Policy, including RoDTEP.

  • Who issues it: The EPC relevant to your product — EEPC India (engineering), FIEO (general), APEDA (agriculture), Textile Export Promotion Council, etc.
  • Fee: ₹2,000–₹5,000/year (varies by EPC)
  • Documents needed: IEC copy, GST certificate, company PAN, product details, two photographs
  • Processing time: 5–10 working days
Pre-Shipment Registration Checklist
  • IEC registered and active on DGFT
  • AD Code registered on ICEGATE (matching your bank branch)
  • RCMC obtained from relevant Export Promotion Council
  • Digital Signature Certificate (DSC) Class 2 or 3 for ICEGATE filings
  • GST registration with export activity enabled
  • PAN card updated with current business name and address

Documents Required for Customs Clearance

Every export or import shipment requires a specific set of documents. Missing or incorrect documents are the number one cause of customs delays.

Export Documents

1. Shipping Bill

The most important document for exports. It is the declaration you file electronically on ICEGATE that triggers the entire customs process.

  • Filed on ICEGATE using your DSC and IEC
  • Contains: IEC, AD Code, HS codes, product description, quantity, value (FOB/CIF), destination country, duty drawback details
  • Types: Standard Shipping Bill (for regular exports), Green Channel Shipping Bill (for low-risk exporters with good compliance history), Bill of Export (for goods moved to a Free Trade Warehousing Zone)
  • Processing: Customs typically processes within 24 hours if documentation is correct

2. Commercial Invoice

Establishes the value of goods. Must match the Shipping Bill exactly in terms of buyer name, product description, quantity, and value.

  • Must include: Seller/buyer details, IEC number, product description, quantity, unit price, total value, currency, Incoterms, country of origin
  • Authorized signatory must sign and stamp

3. Packing List

Details the contents of each package. Customs officers use this to verify cargo during examination.

  • Must include: Number of packages, gross and net weight (in Kgs), dimensions, contents per package, marks and numbers
  • Weight must match Shipping Bill and Bill of Lading

4. Bill of Lading (Sea) / Air Waybill (Air)

Issued by the carrier, this serves as receipt of goods, evidence of the contract of carriage, and (for sea) document of title.

  • Bill of Lading (B/L): For sea freight — original required for LC transactions
  • Air Waybill (AWB): For air freight — non-negotiable

5. Certificate of Origin

Proves goods originate in India. Required for preferential duty rates under Free Trade Agreements (FTAs).

  • Non-Preferential CoO: Issued by Chambers of Commerce (FICCI, CII, local chambers)
  • Preferential CoO: Required under FTAs (India-UAE CEPA, India-Australia ECTA, India-Singapore CECA) for reduced/zero duty in the importing country
  • Apply via eCoO Portal

Import Documents

1. Bill of Entry

The import equivalent of the Shipping Bill. Filed on ICEGATE by the importer or their customs broker.

  • Types: Bill of Entry for Home Consumption (goods cleared into domestic market), Bill of Entry for Warehousing (goods stored in a bonded warehouse), Ex-Bond Bill of Entry (goods released from bonded warehouse)
  • Filed before goods can be cleared from port

2. Import License (if applicable)

Certain products require specific import licenses:

  • DGFT licenses: For restricted items under ITC(HS) classification
  • BIS certification: For products under compulsory BIS standards
  • CDSCO approval: For pharmaceuticals and medical devices
  • WPC license: For wireless/telecom equipment
Documents Checklist
  • Shipping Bill (export) or Bill of Entry (import) — filed on ICEGATE
  • Commercial Invoice (matching Shipping Bill/Bill of Entry exactly)
  • Packing List (weights and contents verified)
  • Bill of Lading / Air Waybill
  • Certificate of Origin (if FTA benefits claimed)
  • Insurance Certificate (for CIF/CIP shipments)
  • Product-specific licenses (BIS, FSSAI, CDSCO as applicable)

The Customs Clearance Process: Step by Step

Here is the end-to-end process your shipment goes through at Indian customs, from filing to release:

  1. Step 1 — File Shipping Bill on ICEGATE: Your customs broker (or you, if filing directly) submits the Shipping Bill electronically via ICEGATE using your DSC. The system auto-validates your IEC, AD Code, and HS codes.
  2. Step 2 — Risk Assessment: Customs' Risk Management System (RMS) automatically classifies your Shipping Bill as Green, Yellow, or Red channel. Green channel shipments skip physical examination. Yellow and Red channel shipments require document verification or physical examination.
  3. Step 3 — Document Scrutiny: For Yellow/Red channel shipments, the customs officer reviews your documents for consistency, correct duty calculation, and regulatory compliance. Faceless Assessment may assign this to an officer at a different location.
  4. Step 4 — Examination (if required): If selected for physical examination, goods are examined at the port by a customs examiner. The examination report is uploaded electronically. You or your CHA must be present (or arrange for someone to be) during examination.
  5. Step 5 — Duty Payment: If any duties are applicable (usually for imports), payment is made electronically through ICEGATE. For exports, this step is typically N/A unless there are specific duty components.
  6. Step 6 — Out of Charge / Let Export Order: Once customs is satisfied, they grant "Out of Charge" (for imports) or "Let Export" order (for exports). This is the final clearance. The Shipping Bill/Bill of Entry is now "passed."
  7. Step 7 — Cargo Movement: For exports, cargo can now be loaded onto the vessel/aircraft. For imports, cargo can be released from the port to the importer's premises.
Timeline

A well-prepared Shipping Bill is typically cleared by customs within 6–24 hours. However, if physical examination is required, add 1–3 working days. For imports, Bill of Entry clearance typically takes 1–2 working days for standard cargo.

ICEGATE and Digital Customs in 2026

ICEGATE (Indian Customs Electronic Commerce / Electronic Data Interchange Gateway) is the national portal for electronic filing of customs documents. In 2026, it handles over 99% of all customs transactions in India.

What You Can Do on ICEGATE

  • File Shipping Bills (export declarations)
  • File Bills of Entry (import declarations)
  • Register AD Codes for your IEC
  • Track BRC status (Bank Realisation Certificate)
  • Track RoDTEP claims and refunds
  • View duty payments and challan status
  • Apply for Green Channel status (if you have a clean compliance record)

Key ICEGATE 2026 Features

  • Faceless Assessment: Your Shipping Bill may be assessed by an officer anywhere in India, not just at your port. This reduces corruption but demands perfect documentation
  • Automated Risk Management: The RMS system uses AI to flag high-risk shipments. Low-risk exporters with clean histories get Green Channel clearance
  • Direct Port Delivery (DPD): For importers with good compliance, containers can be delivered directly from the port without examination, reducing clearance time to hours
  • Duty Payment Integration:electronic payment directly through ICEGATE via net banking, NEFT, or RTGS

Common Delays and How to Avoid Them

Customs delays cost Indian exporters money and credibility. Here are the most common causes and how to prevent them:

1. HS Code Errors

The #1 cause of customs delays. Wrong HS codes lead to wrong duty rates, RoDTEP miscalculation, and potential rejection of your Shipping Bill.

  • Prevention: Verify your HS code on the CBIC website. The first 6 digits are international; digits 7–10 are India-specific. Cross-check with your buyer's import requirements

2. Name Mismatches Across Documents

Your company name, buyer name, and product description must be identical across all documents. Even minor differences ("Pvt Ltd" vs "Private Limited") can trigger a discrepancy.

  • Prevention: Create a master data template for each buyer and use it consistently across all documents

3. Weight and Quantity Discrepancies

If your Packing List says 1,000 Kgs but your Shipping Bill says 980 Kgs, Customs will flag it for physical examination.

  • Prevention: Weigh cargo at your warehouse before sending to port. Ensure all documents reflect the same weight

4. Missing AD Code Registration

You cannot file a Shipping Bill without a registered AD Code. If your bank changed branches or the IFSC code changed, you need to re-register.

  • Prevention: Verify your AD Code is active on ICEGATE before filing. Keep a backup bank branch registered

5. Late Filing

Some filings have strict deadlines:

  • Shipping Bill must be filed before cargo is loaded
  • Bill of Entry must be filed before goods are removed from port
  • BRC must be obtained within 9 months of export date
  • RoDTEP claims must be filed within the stipulated time frame
Penalty Alert

Under the Foreign Trade Policy 2023, penalties for incorrect declarations on Shipping Bills range from ₹10,000 to ₹5,00,000 depending on severity. Repeated violations can lead to suspension of your IEC — effectively halting all your trade activity.

Duty Structure Basics for Imports

Understanding customs duty is essential for pricing your imports correctly and managing your cash flow. Here are the key components:

Basic Customs Duty (BCD)

The primary duty levied on imported goods. Rates vary by product and are specified in the Customs Tariff Act, 1975 and updated through Finance Act notifications.

  • Rates typically range from 0% to 100% depending on the product
  • Check your product's BCD rate on the CBIC website using the HS code
  • BCD can be ad valorem (percentage of value) or specific (per unit/quantity)

Countervailing Duty (CVD)

Levied on goods that are similar to domestically manufactured goods. It is now subsumed under IGST (Integrated Goods and Services Tax) for most products.

  • Replaced by IGST on most imports post-GST
  • Still applies to a few specific items not covered under GST

Special Additional Duty (SAD)

Previously levied on certain imports to protect domestic industry. Now largely abolished post-GST, replaced by IGST and state-level GST components.

IGST (Integrated Goods and Services Tax)

The most significant duty component for most imports. IGST is levied on the assessable value + BCD + any other applicable duties.

  • Formula: IGST = (Assessable Value + BCD + Social Welfare Surcharge) × IGST Rate
  • IGST Rates: Same as domestic GST rates (5%, 12%, 18%, 28%)
  • Key benefit: IGST paid on imports can be claimed as Input Tax Credit (ITC) against your output GST liability

Social Welfare Surcharge (SWS)

A surcharge of 10% on Basic Customs Duty levied on all imports. It replaced the Education Cess and Secondary & Higher Education Cess.

  • Calculated as: 10% of BCD amount
  • Example: If BCD is ₹10,000, SWS is ₹1,000
Quick Calculation Example

For a CIF value of ₹1,00,000 with 10% BCD and 18% IGST: BCD = ₹10,000 → SWS = ₹1,000 → Assessable Value for IGST = ₹1,11,000 → IGST = ₹19,980 → Total Duty = ₹30,980. Your total landed cost is ₹1,30,980.

Anti-Dumping Duty (ADD) and Safeguard Duty

These are additional duties imposed on specific products from specific countries to protect domestic industry:

  • ADD: Levied when imports are priced below "normal value" and cause injury to domestic industry. Common on steel, chemicals, and electronics from China
  • Safeguard Duty: Temporary duty imposed when a sudden surge in imports threatens domestic industry
  • These are product-specific and country-specific. Check the latest CBIC notifications for your HS code
FTA Compliance

If you are importing under an FTA (India-UAE CEPA, India-Australia ECTA, etc.), you may get reduced or zero BCD. However, you must comply with Rules of Origin (ROO) and obtain a valid Certificate of Origin. Non-compliance can result in retroactive duty demand plus penalties.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does customs clearance take in India?
For exports, a well-prepared Shipping Bill is typically cleared within 6–24 hours. If physical examination is required, add 1–3 working days. For imports, Bill of Entry clearance takes 1–2 working days for standard cargo. Direct Port Delivery (DPD) eligible shipments can be cleared in hours. Xecurra's average clearance time is under 12 hours for exports.
Can I file customs documents myself or do I need a customs broker?
You can file Shipping Bills and Bills of Entry yourself on ICEGATE if you have a valid IEC and DSC. However, most MSMEs use a licensed Customs House Agent (CHA) because the process involves complex regulations, HS code classification, and faceless assessment coordination. Xecurra provides licensed CHA services as part of its logistics platform, so you get expert handling without hiring a separate broker.
What happens if my goods are stuck in customs?
If your goods are held up, check the status on ICEGATE for any deficiency memo or examination notice. Common reasons include HS code mismatch, missing documents, or physical examination requirement. Contact your CHA immediately to address the deficiency. Xecurra provides real-time alerts and dedicated support to resolve customs holds within hours, not days. If there is a genuine dispute, we can help file an appeal with the Commissioner of Customs (Appeals).
